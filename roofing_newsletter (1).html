Section B — Retrospective

Lessons from the Ladder

History this industry learned from — or repeats.

The First Roll-Up Wave: What They Got Wrong (Late 1990s–Early 2000s)

The first institutional wave imposed uniform labor models on hyper-local markets and centralized procurement before systems existed to manage it. They paid premium multiples for revenue they couldn't retain post-transition. Result: value destruction, operator exodus after earnout, and a generation of PE skeptics in the trades.

Platforms that survived share one trait: they bought the operator as much as the business — retaining founders with real equity and building tech infrastructure before they needed it.

🎓 Earnout structures that ignore workforce continuity are the sector's #1 value destroyer.

Katrina (2005) & the 2011 Super Outbreak

Katrina: Gulf Coast labor spiked 40–60% in 90 days. Lead times stretched 6+ months. Undercapitalized shops couldn't manage materials, crews, and insurance timelines simultaneously — a demand surge that ruined the unprepared.

2011: 358 tornadoes, $11B insured losses. PE investments in AL/TN/MS in 2012–13 were acquired at peak storm-cycle multiples. When demand normalized, the underlying business was smaller than the thesis assumed.

🎓 Storm EBITDA ≠ recurring EBITDA. Always normalize the trailing twelve.

Technology Spotlight

The Estimating Stack Is Now a Moat

AI and drone adoption have moved from operational upgrade to acquisition differentiator — PE buyers are pricing the gap directly into the multiple.

AI use among roofing contractors jumped from 29% in 2024 to 40% in 2025. Among top operators, drone-enabled inspection and AI estimating have become table stakes. As manufacturers execute coordinated price increases this spring, contractors with tech-enabled estimating workflows — updating bids in real time from live material pricing — hold a structural advantage over those rebuilding quotes manually.

Drone inspection speed

Residential inspection in under 30 min vs. several hours manually. AI classifies hail strikes, membrane blistering, and flashing failures — GPS-tagged and insurance-ready. FAA projects 18% annual growth in drone inspection services.

AI estimating (EagleView, Hover, RoofSnap)

Satellite + drone imagery generates estimates with live material pricing and local labor rates. 70% faster cycle times and higher close rates — critical in the 48-hour post-storm window where March through June is peak hail season across the central U.S.

Predictive maintenance → recurring revenue

AI platforms trigger proactive outreach before roof failure, converting one-time project revenue into service agreements. Recurring revenue is the single most reliable driver of a premium exit multiple in this sector.

Insurance automation

Drone data piped directly into claim workflows provides GPS-verified, time-stamped documentation that speeds payment cycles and reduces supplement leakage — one of the most persistent margin drains on insurance-heavy books.

What it measures: Total daily revenue divided by the number of active crews on site that day. A crew is typically 3–5 workers plus a lead. This single number tells you whether your crews are sized right, jobs sequenced efficiently, and materials arriving on time.

Why PE buyers care: RPCD is a pre-diligence filter. An operator running $8K+ RPCD across 5+ crews has solved three hard problems: labor productivity, job scheduling, and material logistics. Operators below $5K consistently reveal hidden dysfunction — scope creep, rework, and crew downtime that doesn't show up in the P&L until after close.

March 2026 relevance: With Atlas, CertainTeed, and TAMKO executing 5–8% price increases effective late March/April, operators tracking RPCD in real time can catch margin compression at the crew level before it bleeds into quarterly EBITDA. Those without it will discover the damage in May.

Watch-out: High RPCD on residential combined with low commercial exposure can be misleading — residential crews run faster but are more storm-dependent. Normalize for mix before comparing across markets or against a platform peer.

The Ridge Report Bottom Line — March 2026

March delivered a clear message: the consolidation window is compressing from both ends simultaneously. Above the market, QXO closed Kodiak and now controls a $200B+ addressable market with ~$10B still to deploy — and it has named its next targets. Inside the market, Atlas, CertainTeed, and TAMKO executed coordinated 5–8% price increases effective the final week of March, hitting every contractor with open bids and no locked-in material pricing. The operators squeezed hardest are the ones with neither a procurement strategy nor the tech stack to reprice estimates in real time.

Meanwhile, the DOL's move to rescind the 2024 independent contractor rule — confirmed in March's legal roundup — offers PE platforms a potential reprieve on W-2 reclassification exposure. That is not permission to ignore the issue. It is permission to get your documentation in order while the regulatory window is favorable. The rule is pending, not resolved.

The operators entering Q2 in the strongest position are those who spent March doing three things: locking material pricing before April effective dates, tracking RPCD to catch margin compression at the crew level, and closing the gap between their estimating cycle and the market's repricing cycle. Everyone else is reacting. Know your RPCD before a buyer's diligence team does.

Riddle Answer — Page 1

Answer: Pitch. In roofing, pitch (slope) determines drainage and structural integrity — too flat and you get ponding water; steep is better. In private equity, a "pitch" is the investor presentation, and a steeper growth story signals a more compelling multiple. Same word. Entirely different anxieties — and somehow, the same consequences when you get it wrong.
